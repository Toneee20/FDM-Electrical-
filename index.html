How to size your solar system properly

A practical walkthrough — from reading your Eskom bill to choosing the right inverter and battery capacity for South African homes.

The most common mistake South Africans make when buying solar is basing their decision on what a neighbour bought. A 5kW system that works perfectly for a two-bedroom flat will leave a family-of-four without hot water through a Highveld winter.

Step 1 — Read your Eskom bill correctly

Find kWh consumed on your last three bills. Average a winter, summer, and mid-season bill. A typical 3-bedroom home in the Vaal Triangle consumes 450–900 kWh per month. Divide by 30 for your daily kWh figure.

Step 2 — Decide what you want solar to do

  • Load-shedding protection only — 3–5 kWh battery. Budget: R30,000–R65,000 installed.
  • Reduce Eskom bill by 50–70% — 5–8kW inverter, 8–12 kWh battery, 6–10 panels. Budget: R80,000–R145,000 installed.
  • Complete grid independence — 10–15kW inverter, 20–40 kWh battery, 16–28 panels. Budget: R180,000–R350,000+ installed.

Step 3 — Calculate your peak load

Add up everything running at 6pm on a winter weekday. Your inverter must be rated above your peak load with 20–25% headroom for surge currents from motors and compressors.

Step 4 — Size your battery bank

Must cover a full load-shedding block (4–4.5 hours) without going below 20% depth of discharge. Minimum: 3–5 kWh. Comfortable: 10 kWh. Full independence: 20+ kWh.

Step 5 — Size your solar panels

Multiply your daily kWh need by 1.3, then divide by peak sun hours (4–5 hrs for Vaal Triangle). That gives you the panel kW you need.

Comparison5 min read·Decision-stage

Hybrid vs Off-grid vs Grid-tie — which suits you?

The three system types compared on cost, complexity, and load-shedding behaviour. No jargon, just plain answers for South African buyers.

Walk into any solar showroom in South Africa and you will hear three terms used interchangeably: hybrid, off-grid, and grid-tie. They are not the same thing.

Grid-Tie — lowest cost, zero load-shedding protection

Grid-tie inverters shut down the moment Eskom goes down — legally required for safety. Your panels can be shining brilliantly while your house is dark. Typical cost: R25,000–R60,000 for a 3–5kW system. Best for businesses with massive daytime bills in reliable-power areas only.

Hybrid — the sweet spot for most South African homes

Combines solar charger, battery inverter, and grid connection in one unit. Switches to battery during load-shedding seamlessly — usually within 20 milliseconds. Standard FMD recommendation for Vaal Triangle homes. Brands we stock: Sunsynk, Deye, Luxpower, Must. Typical cost: R80,000–R150,000 for a 5–8kW system with 10 kWh battery and 8–10 panels. Return on investment: 4–7 years.

Off-Grid — maximum independence, maximum cost

No Eskom connection at all. Must be engineered for worst-case: shortest day, cloudiest week, highest consumption. Realistic 3-bedroom spec: 10kW inverter, 20–30 kWh battery, 16–24 panels. Cost: R180,000–R400,000+. Right for farms, game lodges, and smallholdings without Eskom supply.

Survival4 min read·Budget-conscious

Load-shedding survival — minimum kit you need

The cheapest practical setup to keep lights, WiFi, fridge and TV running through Stage 6. Real shopping lists at three budget levels.

Stage 6 load-shedding means four-hour blackouts twice a day. A practical setup does not require a full solar installation — here is exactly what you need, in order of priority.

Priority 1 — Lights and WiFi (R8,000–R15,000)

A portable lithium power station (1,000–2,000Wh) with LED lights and your router. Switch to LED globes throughout — 80% less power than incandescent bulbs.

Priority 2 — Save your fridge (R25,000–R50,000)

Minimum: 2–3kW inverter with 3–5 kWh lithium battery. No panels needed yet — battery charges from the wall between outages. Add panels later when budget allows.

Priority 3 — Security system and electric fence

Connect alarm and electric fence to the same inverter-battery as your fridge. A licensed electrician must do this — not a DIY job.

Priority 4 — Work-from-home internet

Router + ONT + laptop = under 150W total. A modest 1kWh battery runs all of that for 6+ hours.

Priority 5 — Hot water (R8,000–R25,000)

  • Gas geyser — R3,500–R8,000 installed. No electricity required.
  • Solar geyser (evacuated tube) — R12,000–R22,000. Ideal for Gauteng's climate. Pays off in 3–4 years.
  • Heat pump — R15,000–R25,000. Most efficient option.
