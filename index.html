How to size your solar system properly

A practical walkthrough — from reading your Eskom bill to choosing the right inverter and battery capacity for South African homes.

Back to all articles

The most common mistake South Africans make when buying solar is basing their decision on what a neighbour bought. A 5kW system that works perfectly for a two-bedroom flat will leave a family-of-four without hot water through a Highveld winter. Right-sizing is not complicated — but it requires twenty minutes with your Eskom bill and an honest look at how your household actually uses electricity.

Step 1 — Read your Eskom bill correctly

Open your last three municipal or Eskom statements and find the line that reads kWh consumed or units used. Take one winter bill, one summer, and one mid-season, then average the three. A typical 3-bedroom home in the Vaal Triangle consumes between 450 and 900 kWh per month. A smallholding with a borehole pump and swimming pool can easily run 1,500 kWh or more. Divide your monthly average by 30 — that is your daily kWh figure, and it drives every decision that follows.

Step 2 — Decide what you want solar to do

There are three realistic goals, and they cost very different amounts:

  • Load-shedding protection only — keep lights, WiFi, fridge, and TV running through Stage 6. You need 3–5 kWh of battery. Budget: R30,000–R65,000 installed.
  • Reduce your Eskom bill by 50–70% — solar generates during the day and charges a battery for evenings. You need a 5–8kW inverter, 8–12 kWh battery, and 6–10 panels. Budget: R80,000–R145,000 installed.
  • Complete grid independence — a 10–15kW inverter, 20–40 kWh battery, and 16–28 panels. Budget: R180,000–R350,000+ installed.

Most suburban homeowners land in category two. It delivers the best return on investment and still covers load-shedding completely.

Step 3 — Calculate your peak load

Walk through your home at the worst-case moment — typically 6pm on a winter weekday. Add up everything running at once: LED lights (200W), TV (120W), WiFi router (15W), fridge (150W), microwave (1,500W), kettle (2,000W). Add 20–30% for motor surge currents. That total is your peak load in watts, and your inverter must be rated above it. If your peak is 4,200W, you need at minimum a 5kW inverter. An electric geyser on the same circuit adds another 2,000–3,000W — most installers recommend a geyser timer or heat pump to keep inverter size and cost manageable.

Step 4 — Size your battery bank

Divide your planned overnight consumption by your battery's usable percentage — 80% for lithium, 50% for lead-acid. If you need 8 kWh of real overnight energy and you are using lithium, you need a 10 kWh nominal battery bank. For two days of autonomy, double it. Our standard recommendation for a 3-bedroom Vaal Triangle home targeting 70% Eskom independence is a 10–15 kWh lithium battery paired with a 5–8kW hybrid inverter.

Step 5 — Size the solar panel array

Gauteng averages 5.5 peak sun hours in summer and 4.5 in winter — better than most South African provinces. A rough rule: you need roughly 1 kW of panels per 1 kWh of battery you want to fully recharge daily. A 10 kWh battery needs at least 10 panels rated 400–545W each. Add 20–30% for shading losses, dust, and panel degradation. For a typical 3-bedroom home, 8–12 panels of 400–545W is the usual starting point.

Don't forget the geyser

Your electric geyser accounts for 30–40% of your monthly electricity bill — a 3,000W element running 2–3 hours per day. The two sensible solutions: a solar geyser or heat pump to remove it from the inverter circuit entirely, or a smart geyser timer that runs it only during peak solar production hours (10am–2pm). Either approach significantly reduces your required battery size and overall system cost.

Always get a written, itemised quote

Any reputable installer will provide a quote specifying inverter make and model, battery brand and chemistry, panel wattage and quantity, and full cable and mounting specifications. If a quote does not include every one of those items, ask for a revised one. A Certificate of Compliance (COC) must also be included in your installation contract — it is a legal requirement and your insurance company can decline a claim without it.

Comparison5 min read·Decision-stage

Hybrid vs Off-grid vs Grid-tie — which suits you?

The three system types compared on cost, complexity, and load-shedding behaviour. No jargon, just plain answers for South African buyers.

Back to all articles

Walk into any solar showroom in South Africa and you will hear three terms used interchangeably: hybrid, off-grid, and grid-tie. They are not the same thing. Getting the distinction wrong can cost you tens of thousands of rands in equipment you either cannot use or should not have bought. Here is a plain-language breakdown of all three.

Grid-Tie — lowest cost, zero load-shedding protection

A grid-tie system connects directly to the Eskom grid and uses solar panels to offset your daytime consumption in real time. When your panels produce more power than you need, the excess is exported back to the grid. The catch: grid-tie inverters are legally required to shut down the moment Eskom goes down — a safety mechanism to protect grid workers. Your panels can be shining brilliantly while your house is dark because the grid is down. This makes a pure grid-tie system a poor choice for most South African homes under the current load-shedding environment.

Typical installed cost: R25,000–R60,000 for a 3–5kW system. Best suited to businesses with massive daytime electricity bills in areas with very reliable power.

Hybrid — the sweet spot for most South African homes

A hybrid inverter combines a solar charger, a battery inverter, and a grid connection in one unit. During a sunny day it runs your home on solar, charges your batteries, and tops up from Eskom only when production falls short. During load-shedding it disconnects from the grid and runs your home on solar and battery — seamlessly, usually within 20 milliseconds. When Eskom returns, it reconnects and resumes charging automatically.

A hybrid system lets you start with a smaller battery bank and add capacity later. You are not over-investing upfront, and you keep your Eskom connection as a genuine backup. For Vaal Triangle homes dealing with Stage 4–6 load-shedding, this is the standard FMD recommendation.

Popular brands we stock and install: Sunsynk, Deye, Luxpower, and Must — all compatible with SA grid standards and most 48V lithium battery banks.

Typical installed cost: R80,000–R150,000 for a 5–8kW hybrid with 10 kWh lithium battery and 8–10 panels. Return on investment: 4–7 years.

Off-Grid — maximum independence, maximum cost

An off-grid system has no Eskom connection at all. You generate, store, and consume entirely your own electricity. The system must be engineered for the worst case: shortest day, cloudiest week, highest consumption moment. That means a significantly larger battery bank, panel array, and inverter than an equivalent hybrid. You also typically need a generator as emergency backup for extended bad weather.

For a typical 3-bedroom SA home, a realistic off-grid spec starts at 10kW inverter capacity, 20–30 kWh battery storage, and 16–24 panels — roughly double the equipment of a well-specified hybrid. For a suburban home in Vereeniging or Three Rivers, off-grid is almost always the wrong financial decision. It makes genuine sense only for rural properties with no grid access, remote smallholdings, or game lodges.

Typical installed cost: R180,000–R350,000+.

Quick comparison

  • Grid-Tie: Lowest cost · No load-shedding protection · Reduces daytime Eskom bill only
  • Hybrid: Mid-range cost · Full load-shedding protection · Expandable · Best ROI · FMD recommendation
  • Off-Grid: Highest cost · No Eskom dependency · Right for rural/no-grid properties only

Lithium vs lead-acid batteries

Whatever system type you choose, lithium (LiFePO4) is now the correct default. It offers 80% usable depth of discharge vs 50% for lead-acid, a 10-year warranty vs 3–5 years, and zero maintenance. We stock Sunsynk, Greenrich, and Hubble lithium batteries — all compatible with the inverters we install.

Survival4 min read·Budget-conscious

Load-shedding survival — minimum kit you need

The cheapest practical setup to keep lights, WiFi, fridge and TV running through Stage 6. Real shopping lists at three budget levels.

Back to all articles

Stage 6 load-shedding means four-hour blackouts twice a day. No hot water, no WiFi, no security system, no way to charge your phone. A practical load-shedding setup does not require a full solar installation — here is exactly what you need, in order of priority, with realistic costs.

Priority 1 — Lights and WiFi (R8,000–R15,000)

The minimum viable setup is a portable lithium power station (1,000–2,000Wh) paired with LED lights and your router. It runs 6 LED bulbs, your WiFi, and keeps phones and laptops charged through a 4-hour outage. These units charge from the wall between outages and need no installation. Also switch to LED globes throughout your home — they draw 80% less power than incandescent bulbs, which stretches your battery capacity enormously.

Priority 2 — Save your fridge and freezer (R25,000–R50,000)

A fridge compressor surges to 3–5x its rated wattage at startup. To run a fridge reliably you need an inverter rated well above 1,000W. The practical minimum for fridge, lights, and WiFi through Stage 6 is a 2–3kW inverter with a 3–5 kWh lithium battery. No solar panels required at this stage — the battery charges from the wall between outages. Panels can be added later when budget allows.

Priority 3 — Security system and electric fence

Load-shedding and opportunistic crime are correlated. Most alarm panels have a 4–8 hour internal battery — electric fence energisers usually have nothing. The fix: connect your security equipment to the same inverter-battery setup as your fridge. Your battery bank can run an alarm panel, energiser, and IP cameras indefinitely. A licensed electrician must move your security circuits to the inverter output — this is not a DIY job.

Priority 4 — Work-from-home internet

A WiFi router draws 15–25W. A fibre ONT box draws 10W. A laptop draws 45–65W. Your entire home office setup draws under 150W total. Even a modest 1kWh battery runs all of that for 6+ hours. If you already have an inverter-battery setup from Priority 2, simply connect your router and laptop to the inverter output.

Priority 5 — Hot water without Eskom (R8,000–R25,000)

Your electric geyser draws 2,000–3,000W for 2–3 hours per day — running it off a battery is expensive. The better alternatives:

  • Gas geyser (instantaneous) — R3,500–R8,000 installed. No electricity required. Hot water on demand. Fully Eskom-independent.
  • Solar geyser (evacuated tube) — R12,000–R22,000 installed. Uses sunlight directly, no inverter involved. Ideal for Gauteng's climate.
  • Heat pump — R15,000–R25,000 installed. Uses 800–1,200W to produce 3–4x that in heat. Most efficient but highest upfront cost.

FMD supplies and installs all three. In the Vereeniging area we most often recommend the evacuated-tube solar geyser as the primary hot water solution.

What NOT to buy

  • Unbranded inverters with no SA support — if it fails, there is no warranty path. Stick to Sunsynk, Deye, Luxpower, Must, or Victron.
  • Lead-acid batteries for a permanent setup — cheap upfront, expensive over time. Only 50% usable capacity, 3–5 year lifespan vs 10 years for lithium.
  • Generators as a primary solution — daily petrol cost, oil changes, noise, and fumes. Keep them as last-resort emergency backup only.

Build it in stages

Start with lights and WiFi. Add a proper inverter-battery for fridge and security. Add solar panels to recharge for free and reduce your Eskom draw. Add a solar geyser or heat pump last. Each stage delivers immediate value — and each one makes the next stage cheaper to add.
